MASTER'S THESIS

 THE INFLUENCE OF PROPER NAMES VERSUS CATEGORY LABEL ON CONCEPT LEARNING

by

PHUC THU UYEN NGUYEN

Supervisor: Dr. Malte R. Henningsen-Schomers


This thesis aims to provide a neurocomputational account for semantic formation and concept learning by training a neurobiologically realistic deep neural network to learn 30 instances from 10 categories, then conducting a subsequent test to assess its performance on categorization, generalization, and the neuronal representations for category-critical versus instance-specific features.

Cell assemblies (CAs) (Braitenberg, 1978) refer to sets of neurons that become highly responsive to conceptually related and repeatedly presented stimuli as a consequence of correlational learning mechanisms. When neurons in the neural network are consistently coactivated and become strongly connected, they form CAs and represent semantic features of a concept. To facilitate readers’ understanding about the results of our CA analyses, I offer in the EXPLORE section an interactive illustration of the CAs emerging in the network model after training. This web application allows readers to explore - on a neuronal representational level - the differential impacts of category labels versus proper names on encoding and storing category-critical and instance-specific features of within-category and across-category instances.

 Background

Linguistic labels - such as proper names or category labels - offer learners the flexibility

to refer to objects as either unique individuals or members of a category.

Over the past decades, developmental psychologists have been consistently demonstrating how category labels facilitate categorization based on the commonalities among category members, while proper names guide individuation through the encoding of instance-specific features (Dewar and Xu, 2007; Scott & Monesson 2009); Althaus and Mareschal, 2014). Seminal experimental observations indicate that labels influence attention to features during object encoding: category labels focus attention to category-specific features, and proper names to unique features of instances (Pickron et al., 2018; LaTourrette and Waxman, 2020). However, neuroimaging findings now suggest that the differential impacts of labelling are indeed more pervasive and complicated on the neuronal representational level than readily observed in the developmental literature. For example, the mere presence of category label can lead to category biases and thus the encoding of category-relevant information despite task goals that require explicit attention to category-irrelevant information (Ashby et al., 2020; Ashby and Zeithamova, 2022). New questions emerge:


HOW DO DIFFERENT TYPES OF LABELS INFLUENCE THE FLEXIBILITY IN OBJECT REPRESENTATIONS?

What is the differential neural and cognitive basis of proper names and category labels during semantic learning and concept formation?

Is the mere presentation of proper names sufficient to override category learning induced by the shared features?

Assessing the concurrent encoding of unique and category features during a learning task was not straightforward within a purely behavioral framework. Brain models are promising technical tools to predict concept learning on a neuronal representational level and describe putative neural mechanisms underlying behavioral observations. In order to achieve that goal, they should be built on architectures that closely model the structural and functional properties of a real brain (Pulvermüller et al., 2021).
