MASTER THESIS

 THE INFLUENCE OF PROPER NAMES VERSUS CATEGORY LABEL ON CONCEPT LEARNING


Linguistic labels - such as proper names or category labels - offer learners the flexibility

to represent objects as either unique individuals or members of a category.

For example, applying consistent categorical labels facilitates the encoding of commonalities among instances of the same category, while applying proper names on each instance increases the encoding of unique features from these instances (Dewar and Xu, 2007; Althaus and Mareschal, 2014; LaTourrette and Waxman, 2020). Despite the evident role in concept learning, neuroimaging data reported category biases driven by category label even in tasks that require attention to unique features, suggesting that the differential impacts of category labels and proper names on concept learning shall not be equated (Ashby et al., 2020; Ashby and Zeithamova, 2022). Following the previous findings, this master thesis aims at answering the following questions:


HOW DO DIFFERENT TYPES OF LABELS INFLUENCE THE FLEXIBILITY IN OBJECT REPRESENTATIONS?

What is the differential neural and cognitive basis of proper names and category labels during semantic learning and concept formation?

Is the mere presentation of proper names sufficient to override category learning induced by the shared features?
